Statutes cannot unsettle vested property rights unless their language necessitates it. This principle is grounded in fairness: the greater the intrusion, the clearer Parliament must speak (the Tunnicliffe principle). When Parliament intervened to reallocate the cost of certain building defects, which ordinarily fall on leaseholders, onto developers through the Building Safety Act 2022 (BSA), it put that principle under strain. The ensuing litigation shows what necessity requires in practice.

Several recent building safety judgments provide useful guidance on when statutory language may displace the presumption against retrospectivity. First, express language makes retrospective effect mandatory. Second, retrospectivity may arise by necessary implication, but only where the statutory scheme includes safeguards capable of mitigating individual unfairness. Third, where a provision is silent and confers no discretionary control, the presumption holds. Two central decisions are now under appeal to the Supreme Court, but they remain clear accounts of how the necessity threshold operates.

## Type A and Type B interference

Not all retrospective legislation raises the same concern. The common law distinguishes between two forms of interference. Type A legislation alters the future consequences of past events. Type B legislation extinguishes rights that have already vested. As Lord Rodger explained in *Wilson v First County Trust (No 2)*, the presumption operates with particular force against Type B interference, because destroying an accrued entitlement is more intrusive than recalibrating future liabilities arising from past conduct. That distinction structures the analysis of the BSA, since the crisis that followed the Grenfell Tower fire compelled Parliament to legislate in ways that engaged both forms of interference.

## Express retrospectivity: s.135

Section 135 extends the limitation period under the Defective Premises Act from six to thirty years and provides that the amendment “is to be treated as always having been in force”.

In *URS Corporation v BDW Trading*, the Supreme Court rejected an attempt to confine the provision to direct DPA claims, holding that only a broader construction would ensure liability lay with those responsible for defective construction. Its conclusion rested on three features.

First, the language: the provision “is to be treated as always having been in force” is express and unequivocal. Second, section 135(5) confirms parliamentary awareness of retrospective operation; human rights safeguards would not be included unless retrospectivity is intended. Third, section 135 was characterised as central to the Act’s remedial scheme, not a peripheral provision where backward effect might be inadvertent. Taken together, clear language and an express safeguard displaced the presumption against retrospectivity with little difficulty.

## Implied retrospectivity with safeguards: s.124

Section 124 empowers tribunals to make Remediation Contribution Orders requiring developers and associated persons to meet “costs incurred or to be incurred” in remedying relevant defects. The provision is silent as to temporal scope.

In *Triathlon Homes v Stratford Village*, the Court of Appeal held unanimously that section 124 operates retrospectively. First, a purely prospective reading would arbitrarily exclude leaseholders who had already paid for remediation, contrary to the Act’s protective purpose. Second, the language — “costs incurred or to be incurred” — naturally encompasses past expenditure. Third, the structure: the “just and equitable” requirement confers discretion to prevent individual unfairness, making retrospective operation workable. Fourth, because section 124 is a Type A interference, since no vested right is extinguished, the presumption against retrospectivity operates with reduced force.

## The limit of implication: Schedule 8

Schedule 8, paragraph 9 provides that a leaseholder “is not liable” to pay service charges in respect of legal or professional costs incurred in connection with relevant defects.

In *Adriatic Land v Long Leaseholders at Hippersley Point*, the Court of Appeal held that paragraph 9 does not apply to costs incurred before 28 June 2022. Three considerations were decisive. First, once incurred, the costs gave rise to an accrued contractual right of reimbursement under the lease. Second, retrospective application would amount to Type B interference, extinguishing a vested entitlement. Third, the provision contains neither express retrospective wording nor any “just and equitable” mechanism permitting case-specific adjustment. Against Type B interference, textual silence could not be displaced by remedial purpose alone.

Nugee LJ dissented, arguing that retrospectivity was justified on these facts. Among other things, he emphasised that the post-Grenfell crisis was precisely the kind of situation where matters had gone seriously wrong enough to justify retrospectivity, and that prospective-only operation would create perverse incentives by encouraging landlords to incur costs quickly before commencement to preserve their reimbursement rights.

This disagreement reflects the Tunnicliffe principle operating as intended: the greater the intrusion into settled rights, the clearer Parliament must speak. The majority concluded that extinguishing accrued entitlements demands unambiguous textual authority; Nugee LJ, weighing the same considerations, reached a different calibration. But both accept the discipline.

## Necessity as discipline

The cases confirm that necessity is not a function of judicial sympathy for legislative aims, but of what Parliament has enacted. Express language compels. Necessary implication, where coupled with safeguards, permits. Silence, even in crisis, does not authorise retrospectivity.

That approach protects the structural role of property rights. Accrued entitlements underpin valuation, lending, and risk allocation; their value depends on predictability. The presumption against retrospectivity does not obstruct urgent reform. Rather it forces Parliament either to speak with explicit clarity or to build in safeguards that permit case-by-case adjustment when it aims to implement rules retroactively. Where neither course is taken, settled rights will prevail.

Whether or not the Supreme Court recalibrates the outcomes in *Triathlon Homes* or *Adriatic Land*, this central lesson of the building safety litigation is unlikely to change.
