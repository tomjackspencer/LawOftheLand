Maureen Comber spent the last years of her life trying to put eighty acres of Broxhead Common back on the commons register. She died before judgment came out. The other week, her claim was dismissed. The state of affairs the judgment describes is, on any fair reading, a mess; and it is one entirely of Parliament's design.

The case itself is a careful piece of statutory construction sitting on top of fifty years of litigation, two House of Lords decisions, and legislation that has been only partially commenced since 2006. To understand why it matters, it helps to start at the beginning.

## Where Common Land Comes From

Common land has its origins, for these purposes, in the medieval manor. The lord owned the soil; his tenants farmed their own plots, and had rights to graze their animals over the uncultivated land left over at the estate's edges. That land was the waste of the manor, and the rights over it were the rights of common.

These rights were not gifts from the lord. They ran with the tenancy. As Lord Denning put it, "no Lord of the Manor could, by selling the manorial lands, deprive the tenants of the manor of their rights of common over them." That is the principle the law has preserved, in one form or another, for at least eight centuries.

Over centuries, much of this land was enclosed; firstly by several private Acts of Parliament, later under the Inclosure Act 1845. By the twentieth century this land remained open ground of real amenity value, even where the old grazing rights had long fallen into disuse.

By the time the Royal Commission on Common Land reported in 1958, it was clear that the public had an interest in seeing this land identified, protected, and preserved. Registration was the first step.

The way Parliament sought to address this was through the Commons Registration Act 1965, which required all common land to be registered by a fixed deadline: 2 January 1970 for applications, 31 July 1970 for finality. Land not registered in time was statutorily deemed not to be common land. The register was to be definitive.

The Act defined common land as two categories: land subject to rights of common, and waste land of a manor not subject to rights of common. Both had to go on the register. No parcel could be both.

## How the Law Got It Wrong

The difficulty was what "waste land of a manor" actually meant once the manorial system had largely ceased to function.

The first important attempt at an answer came from the Court of Appeal in *Re Box Hill Common* [1980] Ch 109, finding that the land had to still belong to the lord of the manor at the time of registration. However, that answer carried a loophole: a landowner who wanted waste off the register need only convey away the lordship and keep the land.

This is precisely what Sir Anthony Milburn sought to do. He sold the lordships of two Hampshire manors to a purchaser and kept the land itself, Hazeley Heath and Mattingley Green. Then he applied to take both off the register, on the ground that he was no longer the lord and the land was therefore no longer waste of a manor. The Court of Appeal, bound by *Box Hill*, agreed.

The House of Lords in a later case did not. In *Hampshire County Council v Milburn* [1991] 1 AC 325, Lord Templeman held that "Parliament cannot have intended that every identifiable piece of waste land which was required to be registered under the Act should cease to be affected by the Act by the voluntary act of the owner for the time being." "Waste land of a manor" meant land of manorial origin, whether or not the formal connection to the lordship had survived. The register existed to protect land, not titles.

The problem with this is that in the period between 1978 and 1990, while *Box Hill* stood, Commons Commissioners had begun cancelling provisional registrations on the strength of the Court of Appeal's judgment. Yet by the time the House of Lords corrected the law, the appeal windows had shut. The cancellations stood anyway.

## The Statutory Fix That Never Arrived

Parliament acknowledged the problem in the Commons Act 2006. Schedule 2, paragraph 4, set up a route back: a fresh application could be made where a Commissioner had refused registration for want of rights of common, without ever asking whether the land might qualify as manorial waste. It answered the *Milburn* problem directly.

But the 2006 Act has never been brought fully into force across England. Only seven "pioneer" authorities and two "2014 authorities" have the Schedule 2 remedies.

The remaining commons registration authorities in England (which include Hampshire) are still operating under the 1965 framework. They are, in the language of the judgment, "1965 Act authorities." For them, the fix Parliament legislated in 2006 does not exist. No timetable for extending it has been published.

That was the position in 2006, and remains the position today.

So why is this relevant?

Broxhead Common in north-east Hampshire was exactly the kind of land the *Milburn* problem affected. In 1974, the Chief Commons Commissioner found that the 80 acres forming part of the historic common were subject to rights of common held by two commoners, Mrs Cooke and Mr Connell.

Although Mrs Cooke's rights were subsequently held to have no valid basis, Mr Connell's survived until a 1978 appeal by the landowner Mr Whitfield. This resulted in a consent order in the Court of Appeal, under which Mr Connell released his rights by deed in exchange for £10,000. As part of the same settlement, Hampshire County Council agreed not to pursue the provisional registration of the 80 acres to final registration.

Nobody, at any stage of those proceedings, had argued that the land was waste of the manor. The rights-of-common case was the one everyone ran. The rights were released. The land was de-registered.

Under *Milburn*, decided thirteen years later, a different outcome might have been possible. If the manorial-waste case had been put to the Commissioner, and if the law had then been as the House of Lords later said it was, the 80 acres might have remained on the register. But it was not put, the law was not yet settled, and by the time both changed it was too late.

## What Was Argued and What Was Decided

Mrs Comber advanced three arguments deserving of comment.

**Jurisdiction.** This argument for Mrs Comber was creative. A later Act can illuminate the construction of an earlier one. Since the 2006 Act was designed to remedy precisely this category of injustice, Parliament cannot rationally have intended that those in pioneer areas should have a remedy while those in Hampshire should not. The court should therefore either read ss.1–3 of the 1965 Act as preserving a residual power to register, or read words into the statute to open a fresh registration window.

Kimblin J rejected this argument. Section 4 of the 1965 Act (the operative provision for making applications) was a time-limited power that expired in 1970 and was then repealed altogether by the 2006 Act. With section 4 gone, there is no power left to receive such an application, and no prescribed form or procedure even if there were.

The scheme of both statutes, as the Court of Appeal held in *R (Littlejohns) v Devon County Council* [2016], is finality. The Schedule 2 remedies carve deliberate exceptions out of that finality, and Parliament's decision not to switch them on in Hampshire is a policy choice, not a drafting slip. The golden rule cures absurd drafting. It does not rewrite a commencement decision. As Kimblin J put it at [65], "the court interprets; it does not legislate."

**Manorial waste.** Even with jurisdiction, the application would have failed on the merits. In 1974 the 80 acres were found subject to rights of common, which put them in the first limb of s.22(1) and out of the second.

The rights were later released by agreement, as part of the very settlement that produced de-registration. Releasing them did not turn the land into manorial waste after the fact. Nobody ran the manorial-waste case before the Commissioner because nobody needed to: the rights-of-common case was the live one, and once it was run the other limb fell away.

Registration was the moment that fixed which limb of s.22(1) applied; nobody put the manorial-waste limb then, and it is too late to put it now.

**Procedural fairness.** The Council asked Mr Whitfield for representations, took new factual material from him about the enclosure, and then decided the application without going back to Mrs Comber. That, Kimblin J held, was unfair: natural justice does not let a decision-maker take fresh evidence from one party and refuse the other any chance to answer it. The Council later conceded that the fences had been put up unlawfully, which rather demonstrated why the second look mattered.

None of which changed the result. With no jurisdiction the outcome was the same whatever the procedure. The finding on fairness is a rebuke without a remedy. As for apparent bias, the Council had instructed the same barrister who resisted Mrs Comber's earlier applications, and it held nearby land as a tenant. Kimblin J rejected the allegation outright.

## A Comment

The judgment is correct on all grounds. However, the position the judgment leaves intact is harder to defend. Parliament identified a discrete class of injustice produced by decisions made on what the House of Lords later confirmed was a wrong understanding of the law. It legislated a remedy. It then implemented that remedy in nine commons registration authorities out of the full complement, and twenty years later has not extended it any further. No timeline has been published. DEFRA was invited to intervene in these proceedings and declined. Kimblin J noted, with considerable restraint, that only DEFRA could explain why the rollout has taken twenty years. That observation was not made lightly.

The 80 acres remain unregistered. The unlawful fences remain standing. And Mrs Comber is no longer here to push her case further.

The law may be clear, but the outcome appears highly unsatisfactory.
