The Ibis next to Wembley Stadium, a 43-metre, 210-bedroom tower, has been empty since July 2025. The problem is that almost the whole thing is wrapped in the same kind of cladding that caused the tragedy at Grenfell. Clearly this needs fixing, but the more complicated issue is who should pay.

For residential buildings this question is often simple, because the Building Safety Act governs this problem. However, that only helps when it comes to dwellings — a hotel is not a dwelling.

This was precisely the problem the High Court had to grapple with in *Essendi UK Hotels 2 Ltd v London Property Company Ltd*.

In essence, the judge held that the landlord must remove and replace the cladding at its own cost. The outcome feels fine (my Coasean readers will agree it's legal certainty that matters, and once a rule is in place parties can bargain round it). However, from a legal perspective, it is noteworthy how the court got there.

I've already mentioned that the Building Safety Act does not apply to hotels; however, this doesn't leave buildings without protection, as it is where the Fire Safety Order 2005 comes in. This requires a responsible person to assess fire risk and reduce dangerous risks so far as reasonably practicable. Moreover, this Order has some teeth: the London Fire Brigade had already served a deficiencies notice requiring the cladding to be dealt with within twelve months, with prohibition notices and prosecution available if that didn't happen.

Unfortunately for Ibis, Article 39 of the Order specifies that nothing in the Order gives rise to a civil right of action, as enforcement sits with the fire authority and the criminal courts. The regime can therefore compel the work, but gives the tenant very few means to make that happen.

However, Ibis wanted to make their landlord fix the building so they could re-open their hotel. The High Court's judgment is therefore interesting for the two ways it tried to fill this gap.

The first is straightforward. The lease had a "legal obligations" covenant: a promise by the landlord to carry out whatever works are required by any statutory obligation affecting the building. The Fire Safety Order is a statutory obligation affecting the building, so it walks straight into the contract through that door. Article 39 stops the tenant suing on the Order itself, but it doesn't stop the tenant suing the landlord for breaching its promise to comply with it.

The second part is doing a lot more work, and is more controversial. This arose because the lease required the landlord to keep the structure and cladding in "good and substantial repair and condition". Accordingly, Essendi argued that this clause on its own required the recladding.

The conventional view is that a repair-and-condition covenant is about physical state and assumes some deterioration from an earlier condition. Here the panels were physically intact. In *Alker v Collingwood Housing Association*, a tenant put her arm through the glass panel in her front door and was badly hurt. The glass was ordinary glass rather than safety glass, but it was sound: intact, undamaged, and probably compliant with the building regulations when it went in. It had merely always been dangerous.

The Court of Appeal held that repair, maintenance and good condition were all aimed at physical state, and none of them reached a thing that was hazardous but undamaged. A duty to put premises into good condition, the court held, still does not amount to a duty to put them into a safe one. If the panels here are sound but lethal, *Alker* reads less like a case to be distinguished and more like the answer.

Yet the court looked past this, treating the physical-damage requirement as a presumption and then displacing it. A tall hotel let after Grenfell, he held, carries an obligation to be kept reasonably fit for that use as regards fire safety, including any latent defects. The effect, bluntly, is that the same words in the lease can mean different things depending on what's happened in the world since it was signed.

There is good reason to be uneasy about reaching that result here. The protections built after Grenfell were designed around a particular figure: the residential leaseholder, living behind cladding that could kill, with no power to compel its removal and a service charge that could be turned against them to fund the work. If Parliament wished to extend the Building Safety Act to hotels, it was perfectly within its rights to do so, but it legislated only for dwellings.

Indeed, there's reason to keep it at dwellings: there is a significant power imbalance between individual leaseholders and managing agents in a way that does not exist with commercial parties. Ibis may be suffering because their hotel is unsafe, but they always have the means to remedy the situation themselves, either by cashing in insurance policies or out of their own funds. Extending established precedent from a higher court to deal with this situation is both to remedy mere commercial loss and to put the High Court in an uncomfortable place.
