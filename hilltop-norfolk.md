Imagine you run an outdoor education centre where children come from across the country to get muddy for a week, and your entire business model depends on the peace and quiet of the Norfolk countryside.

Then one day, you look out of the window and notice that the local council wants to build a household waste recycling centre about 35 metres from your playing field.

That is more or less what happened to Hilltop Experiences. Determined to stop it, they took the council all the way to the Court of Appeal in a case that touches on many fundamental questions in English planning law: when must a council look for alternative sites? What happens when an officer gets a fact wrong? And how badly does an officer's report have to mislead a planning committee before a court will quash the permission?

## The Project: More Than Just a Dump

Norfolk County Council's proposal, universally referred to in the proceedings as a "household waste recycling centre" (HWRC: to normal people, a tip), was rather more than somewhere to drop off old paint tins. Alongside the concrete pad and the skips, the application included things like a staff welfare office, a reuse shop with solar panels on its roof, and what the planning documents cheerfully described as "ancillary small-scale sale of non-recycled items (Christmas trees, logs, compost bins and green waste sacks)".

So not just a dump. More of a community recycling and retail hub, powered by solar energy, surrounded by living willow acoustic fencing, delivering voluntary biodiversity net gain of 11.76% in habitat units. The legal requirement didn't apply to this application, but they exceeded it anyway.

The existing tip, a modest 0.1 hectares sitting in a layby off the A148, had been identified as no longer fit for purpose back in 2015. Only eight cars could fit on site at once, meaning traffic regularly backed up onto the main road. The new site would be 0.45 hectares, with a proper T-junction and capacity for 6,000 tonnes of waste per annum.

There was one significant complication. Both the existing HWRC and the proposed replacement sat within the Norfolk Coast National Landscape ("NCNL"), formerly an Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty.

Sitting just outside that protected landscape, roughly 35 metres from the proposed site, was Hilltop Experiences: a 33-acre outdoor education centre visited by around 10,000 children each year. Unsurprisingly, they did not support the development. Nor did North Norfolk District Council, the Norfolk Coast Partnership, the CPRE, Beeston Regis Parish Council, and a number of schools whose pupils attended Hilltop. Twelve formal objections were raised in total.

## The Rules: What Was Required

English planning law requires authorities to determine applications in accordance with the development plan unless material considerations indicate otherwise (s.38(6), Planning and Compulsory Purchase Act 2004).

The relevant policy here was Policy EN1 of the North Norfolk Local Development Framework Core Strategy, which covered the NCNL. It said, in terms:

> Proposals that have an adverse effect will not be permitted unless it can be demonstrated that they cannot be located on alternative sites that would cause less harm.

Essentially, if you want to build something "harmful" in the National Landscape, you must first show you looked for somewhere else.

Above the development plan also sits the National Planning Policy Framework, which gives National Landscapes (formerly AONBs) the highest level of landscape protection and requires major development within them to be refused unless there are exceptional circumstances and the development is demonstrably in the public interest.

However, the council's officers determined that this proposal was not "major development", a conclusion the CPRE, and later Hilltop, contested.

As for the alternatives duty: the courts have drawn a distinction between a decision-maker who is merely permitted to consider alternatives and one who is compelled to: *Derbyshire Dales DC v Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government* [2009] EWHC 1729.

If the duty is mandatory, failing to discharge it properly is an error of law. If discretionary, the court will defer. Here, with Policy EN1 expressly requiring an alternatives exercise, there was no dispute that the duty was mandatory. The question was how thoroughly the assessment had to be done.

## What Actually Happened

The site search had a troubled history. In 2017, consultants NPS Property identified two potential alternative sites, both of which were discounted. In 2019, NPS were instructed again. Three more options were explored: one was ruled out as mature woodland; one was a quarry whose owner, per the planning supporting statement, "did not engage"; and the third became the application site. By 2020 a lease was being negotiated, and the planning application was submitted in February 2023, six years after the original site assessment.

The council's own officers were uncomfortable. Their committee report acknowledged frankly that the site selection exercise was inadequate, that they had not even been provided with a copy of the 2019 assessment, and that they could not "categorically say that no land was available outside of the National Landscape". They recommended treating the application as a departure from the development plan.

And yet they also recommended granting permission, on the basis that public interest outweighed the policy conflict. The committee agreed, and permission was granted in June 2024, subject to 26 conditions including one requiring the old tip to be returned to woodland once the new site opened.

## East Beckham Quarry

After the first hearing before Lieven J but before judgment was handed down, Hilltop's owner received a phone call from a Mr Robert Batt, who had seen a newspaper article about the proceedings. Mr Batt owned East Beckham Quarry (also known as the Gresham Gravel site), located 600 metres southwest of the proposed HWRC and, crucially, outside the NCNL.

Mr Batt said he had tried to engage with the council's consultants in 2019, expressing interest in hosting the HWRC and offering to phase his quarrying accordingly. Consultants had chased him for a meeting through autumn 2019, but none was ever arranged. In May 2021 he emailed again asking if the council was still interested. He received no reply.

Two things then became clear. First, the case officer, Mr Zieja, had understood the planning statement's reference to a quarry whose owner "did not engage" to mean Beeston Regis Quarry, a different quarry just 300 metres east. He had no idea the reference was to East Beckham Quarry, which was outside the NCNL. Second, the officer's report had told the committee that all three 2019 alternatives were "seemingly in the National Landscape". That was wrong.

Hilltop applied to adduce this evidence and to amend their pleadings to add a material error of fact ground.

## The Courts

Before the High Court, Hilltop advanced six grounds: that the alternatives exercise was legally inadequate; that the council had taken irrelevant considerations into account in deciding the proposal was not "major development"; that it had failed to properly apply the NPPF test for development in a National Landscape; an Equality Act ground relating to the children with protected characteristics who used the site; and a challenge to the EIA screening opinion. A fifth ground was withdrawn before the hearing. After the hearing but before judgment, Hilltop applied to adduce the East Beckham Quarry evidence and amend their pleadings to add a material error of fact ground.

Lieven J dismissed everything. The alternatives exercise was legally adequate: the officer's report had openly acknowledged its limitations, told members other sites might exist, and recommended permission on public interest grounds regardless. That was rational, not unlawful. The remaining grounds each failed on their own terms. The East Beckham Quarry evidence was refused as immaterial for the same reason as the main alternatives ground; namely, that members already knew sites might exist outside the NCNL.

At the Court of Appeal, Hilltop did not pursue most of the original grounds. The appeal was confined to two, both arising from the East Beckham Quarry material: first, that the officer's report had materially misled the committee by failing to identify it as a potentially available site outside the NCNL; second, that the council had made a material error of fact in treating all three 2019 alternatives as being within the National Landscape.

The Court of Appeal agreed with the High Court. On the misleading officer's report argument, it was found that the report could not have misled the committee on the question of alternative sites because it had explicitly told them that other sites might exist and counted that as a factor against permission. Naming East Beckham Quarry specifically could not have changed anything.

On the error of fact argument, Mr Zieja's confusion between the two quarries was accepted as a genuine error, but it failed the materiality test. The committee had already been told that suitable alternatives outside the NCNL might exist and had granted permission on that basis. Identifying one specific candidate site could not change the reasoning of a decision that had already accounted for that possibility.

## What the Case Shows

Norfolk County Council gets its recycling centre, complete with its willow fence, solar reuse shop, and condition requiring the old tip to become woodland. Hilltop does not get its quashing order.

The result may feel unsatisfying to some. But the logic is sound. Planning law does not require perfection: it requires rationality, honesty, and genuine engagement with the issues. The officers here were candid about what they didn't know, the committee had what it needed to decide lawfully, and the planning balance, however finely struck, was within the range of reasonable outcomes.

But step back from the legal analysis and the story is a striking one. Norfolk needed a bigger tip. It already had a tip. The new one would go 35 metres from the old one. It took nine years from identifying the need to get permission, two sets of court proceedings, and a Court of Appeal judgment to get there. And even then, it turned out a landowner outside the protected area had twice offered his land and never received a reply.
